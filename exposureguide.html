EXPOSURE GUIDE

Aperture

Aperture can be defined as the opening in a lens through which light passes to enter the camera. It is expressed in f-numbers like f/1.4, f/2, f/2.8 to express the size of the lens opening, which can be controlled through the camera.

Shutter Speed

Shutter speed is the length of time camera shutter is open, exposing light onto the camera sensor. A fast shutter speed means objects would be frozen while a slow shutter speed shows more movement.

ISO

ISO in a camera option that will either brighten or darken your photography. Increasing the ISO number will make your pictures brighter and help you capture good shots in darker environments.

APERTURE NUMBERS

F/1.4


F/1.4 is the smallest possible number of aperture but is actually the biggest in terms of how wide your lens will open. Taking photos of something like tiny stars would be the best example for this as you will need a lot of light and time for the camera to sense all the stars and get a good photo.

F/2


F/2 lets half as much light in as compared to f/1.4. You will use this to create a very thin depth of field meaning that it could be great for taking portrait photos of a single person and it will blur the background out and put the subject into focus.

F/4


F/4 is a moderate aperture with a moderate depth of field too. It can be used when you have a bit more objects in foreground like the bird and a tree in this example. This will still work like f/2 but as there are more objects in the front you will need a bigger number to get all the things in focus.

F/8



F/8 is a moderate number with a moderately large depth of field meaning that it is a good option for landscape photography such as mountains and cityscapes. You will have most things in focus too and still capture all the details that you want.

F/11



F/11 is another good option to capture extra details like patterns in architecture or focusing on an object that is far away and still managing to capture it. This will also give a moderately large depth of field and some blur but most most foreground objects will be visible.

F/16



F/16 is a small aperture but provides the most depth of field out of all of these options.If you use a very small aperture like f/16 would mean that you can actually capture both background and foreground objects in perfect focus without worrying about blurring.
